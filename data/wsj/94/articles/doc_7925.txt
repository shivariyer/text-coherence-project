Stocks retreated for a second straight day as worrisome technical signs inspired more profit-taking.
Economically sensitive issues, which managed to avoid the selling Monday, were among the biggest decliners.
Bethlehem Steel dropped 2 1/8 to 21 5/8, Caterpillar fell 1 5/8 to 98 5/8 and AlliedSignal slipped 1 1/8 to 77 7/8.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average posted its biggest loss of the year, sliding 17.45, or 0.45%, to 3895.34.
It dipped below the 3900 mark for the first time since its initial move over that level Friday.
Most broader indexes fell somewhat less than the industrial average.
Declining issues led advancers 1,202 to 883 on the New York Stock Exchange.
Volume on the exchange was active at about 326 million shares, up from 296.9 million shares Monday.
Although the Dow industrials have set a series of records and jumped about 4% in the year to date, most broad-based indexes are up only about 1%.
The Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index, meanwhile, has only set one new high.
The S&P 500 lost 1.05, or 0.22%, to 470.92, and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index fell 0.62, or 0.24%, to 261.13.
The Nasdaq Composite Index slumped 4.26, or 0.54%, to 786.39.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index slid 2.78, or 0.58%, to 480.59.
William Dodge, market strategist at Dean Witter Reynolds, said some investors went bargain-hunting in consumer nondurable groups, such as drug makers.
Pfizer added 1/2 to 62, Johnson & Johnson rallied 1/2 to 40 7/8, and Schering-Plough rose 1/2 to 62 5/8.
Merck jumped 1 to 35 3/4 after the pharmaceuticals giant in Whitehouse Station, N.J., recorded fourth-quarter net income of 56 cents a share, up from 53 cents last year.
International Business Machines was in the spotlight much of yesterday after its stock tumbled on its late morning earnings release.
Big Blue's shares skidded as low as 55 3/8 before recovering late in the day to finish at 58 1/4, off just 3/8.
As expected, the troubled Armonk, N.Y., computer maker posted its first quarterly profit in more than a year, but investors were initially concerned about lower-than-expected revenue in the period.
They were reassured later in the day when IBM said it likely will stay out of the red in the first quarter.
Elsewhere, Cooper Industries plunged 9 7/8, or 19%, to 41 5/8.
The company reported fourth-quarter earnings of 78 cents a share, up from 73 cents a share last year, but below the 81 cents expected by analysts surveyed by IBES, the institutional Brokers Estimate System.
Consortium Grupo Dina skidded 6 3/8, or 21%, to 23 7/8 after the Mexican truck maker said its earnings fell to 24.7 million pesos ($8 million) in the fourth quarter from 85 million pesos a year ago.
It said truck sales fell 10% for all of 1993.
American Express added 1 1/4 to 32 3/4.
The New York financial services company late Monday reported fourth-quarter earnings of 78 cents a share, up from 15 cents a share a year earlier, and released details of its long-anticipated plan to spin off its Lehman Brothers unit.
Dean Witter Discover lost 5/8 to 37 3/4.
The New York brokerage and credit card firm earned 81 cents a share in the fourth quarter, up from 51 cents a share a year ago, but just below the 82 cents mean estimate of analysts surveyed by IBES.
Airline stocks slipped as major carriers said they will match fare cuts initiated by Trans World Airlines.
AMR, the parent of American Airlines, dropped 1 1/8 to 68 3/8; Delta Air Lines lost 1 to 53 7/8 and Southwest Airlines declined 7/8 to 36 1/8.
Spectrum Information skidded 1 to 6 5/16 on the Nasdaq Stock Market after CNBC cable television reported rumors that former Apple Computer Chief Executive Officer John Sculley was resigning from the top positions at Spectrum, Manhasset, N.Y., and that the company was the subject of a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation.
Mr. Sculley denied both rumors, but the company said last night that it was, in fact, the subject of an SEC probe centering on the value of its licensing agreements.
Chevron was off 1/8 to 92 1/2, Mobil eased 3/8 to 79 7/8 and Exxon added 1/8 to 66 1/4 after each reported improved earnings from operations.
But oilfield-service stocks tumbled.
Baker Hughes lost 1 1/4 to 20 1/4, Schlumberger dropped 1 3/8 to 57 1/4, and Halliburton slipped 1 3/8 to 31 5/8.
On Nasdaq, American depositary receipts of L.M. Ericsson, a Swedish telecommunications company, lost 2 1/4 to 43 3/4 after Goldman Sachs downgraded the issue to "market underperformer" from "market performer."
Cheyenne Software climbed 1 1/8 to 33 3/8 on the Amex.
The company in Roslyn, N.Y., reported earnings of 33 cents a share for the second quarter ended Dec. 31, up from 17 cents a share a year earlier.
Economically sensitive stocks have provided much of the fuel for the rocketing Dow industrials.
That market sector finally lured profit-takers yesterday.
Ricky Harrington, a technical analyst at Interstate/Johnson Lane, said many investors are becoming worried about the narrow nature of the market's recent gains.
Analysts often view such limited strength as a sign of technical weakness.
