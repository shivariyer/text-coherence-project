A disappointing two-year note auction caused small-company stock investors to abandon an early buying trend and switch to a selling mode as bonds slid.
Two key indexes were mildly lower, while big cyclical issues were hit hard.
The Russell 2000 Index of small-capitalization stocks fell 0.13, or 0.05%, to 264.92, but above its session low of 264.52.
On the Nasdaq Stock Market, the Composite Index dropped 2.04, or 0.26%, to end at 789.11.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average retreated about 20 points to 3891.68.
Losing Nasdaq issues surpassed gainers, 1,570 to 1,449, on Nasdaq National Market volume of 254,449,700 shares and total volume of 294,656,500.
Tuesday, National Market volume was just 221,117,300 shares and overall volume was only 263,268,500.
Among financial stocks, Summit Bancorp., Chatham, N.J., fell 1 3/8 to 20 1/8.
Tuesday, the company said it has entered into a definitive agreement to acquire Crestmont Financial in a tax-free exchange of stock.
Summit said it expects to incur a one-time restructuring charge of about $11 million after taxes this year because of the acquisition.
Crestmont of Edison, N.J., fell 1 3/8 to 19 7/8.
Summit agreed to sell to Lehman Brothers about $100 million of Crestmont's performing and nonperfroming commercial real-estate loans, including all of Crestmont's nonperforming real-estate assets.
America Online rose 6 3/4 to 80 1/4.
The computer on-line information-service company said it has increased its number of total "ports," or connections for individual users, by about 20% in the past several weeks.
The Vienna, Va., company informed subscribers on Feb. 1 that it would limit access during peak hours because of a capacity shortage.
Collagen closed 1 1/8 higher at 23 1/8.
The Palo Alto, Calif., biomedical firm said its board has authorized the purchase of 300,000 common shares in the open market.
Also, National Record Mart zoomed up 1 to 5 3/4.
The Carnegie, Pa., operator of home-entertainment product retail stores said its board approved a program to buy back as much as $1 million in common shares from time to time.
Lower fiscal second-quarter earnings caused WCT Communications to plunge 1 3/8 to 4 3/4.
The Santa Barbara, Calif., telecommunications company said its net income for the period fell to two cents a share from five cents a year earlier.
In addition, Hancock Institutional Equity Services cut its rating on WCT to "hold" from "buy," and Oppenheimer & Co. downgraded the stock to "market performer" from "buy."
Abrams Industries dropped 1 1/4 to 6 1/4.
The Atlanta construction company posted a fiscal third-quarter loss of $102,370, vs. a profit of $23,835 the year before.
Meanwhile, improved earnings helped Marsam Pharmaceuticals climb 1 7/8 to 15.
The Cherry Hill, N.J., company said its fourth-quarter profit was five cents a share, up sharply from a loss of 30 cents last year.
Oshkosh B'Gosh fell 1 5/8 to 15 1/8.
The Oshkosh, Wis., casual clothing company said its fourth-quarter loss widened to 42 cents a share on a charge, compared with three cents a year ago.
New issue Equity Inns jumped to 12 3/4, above its $10-a-share offering price this morning.
The Memphis, Tenn., real-estate investment trust said proceeds will go toward debt repayment, the acquisition of properties, working capital and acquisitions.
Arden Industrial Products rose to 13 1/2 from its offering price of $12 a share.
The St. Paul, Minn., distributor of fasteners said proceeds will be used to finance the opening of new distribution centers, debt repayment and general corporate purposes.
On the American Stock Exchange, Fibreboard, a Walnut Creek, Calif., insulation company, jumped 2 3/8 to 37 1/2.
USA Today reported that a group led by Carl Pohlad, the owner of the Minnesota Twins major league baseball team, is weighing a bid for Fibreboard.
Fibreboard wasn't immediately available for comment.
Christine Callies, a market analyst at Brown Brothers Harriman, said smaller stocks have been outdoing their larger counterparts for the last week after underperforming bigger stocks for months.
"This is to be expected," she said.
"After the market has experienced a sharp smack, and then starts to stabilize, investors begin to think about returning to small stocks to give them leverage for the advance.
This is usually a short-term phenomenon."
John Avignone, head of over-the-counter trading at Conning & Co., said market players continue to talk about the possibility that the Federal Reserve will again raise short-term rates.
Although he said "the fear is overdone," Mr. Avignone added that climbing rates are on everyone's minds.
He said part of today's decline might have been a delayed reaction to Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan's testimony Tuesday before Congress.
Greenspan said the Fed will likely raise short-term rates but didn't say when the move might occur.
Rate-sensitive stocks drew selling as bond prices declined.
Americredit, Fort Worth, Texas, lost 1/2 to 6 1/8 on the Big Board; Glendale Federal Bank, Glendale, Calif., fell 3/8 to 5 7/8, also on the New York Stock Exchange; and BSB Bancorp, Binghamton, N.Y., declined 3/4 to 22 1/2.
