A futures-related sell program sent stock prices lower at the close, despite a bond rally amid news of another Federal Reserve tightening.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average declined 2.30, or 0.06%, to 3862.55 after posting moderate gains earlier as government securities surged.
Investors had hesitated to make big moves as they awaited word from the Fed's Federal Open Market Committee, a policy-setting body, which met yesterday.
Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan announced a decision to increase pressure on reserve positions, linked to an increase of 1/4-percentage point in short-term rates.
Speculation Monday that a 1/2-percentage point boost to the discount rate was imminent sparked selling that lopped 30.80 points from the Dow industrials.
Mr. Greenspan's announcement soothed bond-market fears that this worst-case scenario would be played out.
The Treasury's long bond jumped 1 1/4 points, and its yield fell to 6.85%.
Observers said short covering aided the advance.
The Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index gained 0.26 to 468.80, and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index added 0.33 to 260.24.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index gained 0.96 to 471.73.
Big Board advancers led decliners, 1,189 to 925, according to a preliminary count.
Big Board volume stood at 282,241,000 shares, up from 247,358,000 Monday.
The Fed last boosted rates on Feb. 4, causing a steep slide in equity prices.
The Dow industrials sank 96 points that day.
Market strategists say that sell-off followed strong gains in January that made stocks ripe for a correction.
Yesterday's Fed move was widely expected and already largely discounted by stock investors.
Rate-sensitive financial issues rose yesterday.
Federal National Mortgage Association lifted 1 3/4 to 83 1/8, and First Chicago added 3/4 to 51 1/4.
First Interstate Bancorp rose 1 5/8 to 74 1/2.
The company said its management has proposed a significant increase in its quarterly dividend and its 1994 buyback program to eight million shares from the previously announced 1.5 million.
Among blue chips, Philip Morris was a notable loser, down 1 7/8 to 52 1/8 on news that a House health subcommittee voted to raise federal cigarette taxes by $1.25 a pack.
RJR Nabisco Holdings fell 1/2 to 6 1/8.
Another component of the Dow industrials, Merck, lost 1 1/2 to 30 1/2.
Yesterday's Wall Street Journal reported that Sandoz Pharmaceuticals plans to launch its cholesterol-reducing drug at about half the price of competing products made by Merck and Bristol-Myers Squibb, which fell 1 3/8 to 53 1/8.
Furman Selz and Hambrecht & Quist both lowered their investment recommendations on Merck.
Other health-care stocks were also weak.
Eli Lilly sagged 2 5/8 to 53 1/2; Pfizer retreated 1 to 57; and Schering-Plough declined 2 to 57 1/2.
Nasdaq stocks were hurt by weakness in some large-capitalization technology issues.
The Nasdaq Composite Index extended Monday's 6.63-point loss by 0.96 to end at 796.34.
Nasdaq-traded Novell, of Provo, Utah, slid 3 3/4 to 20, as analysts took a dim view of the company's plan to acquire WordPerfect and Borland International's Quattro Pro spreadsheet.
SoundView Financial, Cowen, Merrill Lynch, S.G. Warburg Group, UBS Securities, Alex. Brown & Sons, First Boston and Dean Witter all cut their ratings on Novell.
Meanwhile, Borland, of Scotts Valley, Calif., lost 7/8 to 13 1/4 on Nasdaq.
American depositary receipts of some overseas telecommunications concerns regrouped after sliding Monday.
Telefonica de Argentina rebounded 4 1/4 to 68, and Hong Kong Telecommunications gained 2 3/8 to 51 1/4.
Telefonos de Mexico surged 6 1/4 to 65 and topped the Big Board most active list with volume of 9.4 million shares, compared with average daily turnover of 4.3 million.
Other Mexican stocks rallied as the Mexican Stock Exchange soared on news that the ruling party's biggest political threat said he won't run for president this August.
ADRs of Grupo Casa Autrey, a distributor of pharmaceuticals, beauty and consumer products, rose 3 3/8 to 26 5/8; Grupo Financiero Serfin lifted 2 3/8 to 26 3/8 and Consorcio G Grupo Dina, a manufacturer of buses and vehicle-replacement parts, climbed 1 3/4 to 18 1/8.
Among new issues, JDN Realty climbed to 23 1/8 from its offering price of $22 a share late Monday.
The Atlanta developer of Wal-Mart-anchored shopping centers said it will use proceeds to acquire properties and repay debt.
"The positive is that the market didn't go down," said John Wosczyna, a market strategist at Roney & Co.
"But the worry is why didn't we get a bounce if it was already built in?
The Fed didn't jack the rates up that high, so stocks will be vulnerable to a correction."
Greg Kuhn, who heads Kuhn Asset Management, said caution still exists that the discount rate could still be pushed higher.
"We saw a bit of a relief move higher earlier, but this could be just the beginning in terms of jitters about rates," he said.
