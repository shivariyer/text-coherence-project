Blue-chip issues led the stock market higher, boosted by rebounding bond prices and program buying.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 25.89 points, or 0.66%, to 3931.92.
Since the Federal Reserve announced Friday that it will tighten credit conditions, the industrials have taken back about 60 points of the 96 lost that day.
Among big cyclicals, AlliedSignal rose 2 1/2 to 79 7/8; Procter & Gamble gained 2 1/4 to 59 1/4; and McDonald's added 1 1/4 to 60 3/8.
Broader indexes also rose.
The Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index moved up 1.72 points, or 0.37%, to 472.77, and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index added 0.94 points, or 0.36%, to 262.90.
Big Board advancers led decliners, 1,289 to 850, on volume of 332,600,000 shares, up from 318,040,000 shares on Tuesday.
The average price per share rose 12 cents.
Two bouts of futures-related programs -- one yesterday morning and another right before the close -- aided the advance in the equity market.
The Nasdaq Composite Index rose with the help of firm technology issues.
It jumped 3.83 points, or 0.49%, to 786.53.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose for a second straight day, gaining 2.91, or 0.61%, to 480.59.
A better-than-expected January book-to-bill report released after the market close Tuesday boosted some chip makers.
The semiconductor industry report shows the ratio rose to 1.07 last month from 1.04 in December.
That means that for the latest month, the industry received $107 of new orders for every $100 of chips shipped.
Among chip stocks, Atmel (Nasdaq) rose 1 7/8 to 42 3/8; Intel (Nasdaq) inched up 3/8 to 63 7/8; and Analog Devices Inc. gained 7/8 to 27 3/8.
Also in the technology group, Microsoft (Nasdaq), Redmond, Wash., rose 7/8 to 79 5/8.
William Gates, the company's chairman, said the software giant could double its revenue by fiscal 1997.
Compaq Computer moved up 2 1/8 to 90.
Compaq's vice president of corporate development, Robert W. Stearns, said the Houston company's growth pace is on course for Compaq to overtake International Business Machines as world-wide leader in personal-computer shipments by 1996.
IBM declined 1/2 to 53 1/8.
Computer Sciences, El Segundo, Calif., gained 2 to 37 1/2.
Merrill Lynch named the computer-based systems and communications systems company its Focus One Stock of the Week.
CUC International, a Stamford, Conn., provider of membership-based consumer services, leaped up 2 1/2 to 31 7/8.
Bear Stearns analyst Steve Kernkraut upgraded the company's shares to "strong buy" from "buy."
A notable loser was WMX Technologies, an Oak Brook, Ill., provider of solid-waste management services, which sagged 3 1/4 to 25 as fourth-quarter earnings slipped to 34 cents a share from 37 cents a year earlier.
Wall Street had been expecting earnings of 39 cents a share.
WMX led the Big Board's most-actives list with volume of 15 million shares, above average daily turnover of 1.5 million.
Ford Motor fell 1 to 68 1/2, despite posting stellar fourth-quarter earnings of $1.30 a share, compared with a loss of $1.85 a share on charges.
Analysts said the market response reflects a perception that the Big Three auto makers are among the biggest beneficiaries of the rebounding U.S. economy and that after the past few quarters, higher-than-expected earnings have come to be expected.
New issue Istituto Mobiliare Italiano jumped to 23 5/8 from its offering price of $19.24 an American depositary receipt yesterday.
Each ADR represents three common shares.
Proceeds from the sale of the Rome-based state-controlled merchant bank and financial services group issue will go to Italy's treasury.
Western National climbed to 13 3/8, above its initial offering price of $12 a share yesterday.
The Houston insurance company said its portion of the net proceeds will be about $21.4 million and will be used for general corporate purposes.
Traders said investors temporarily shelved their concerns about interest rates with the Treasury's long bond jumping more than 1/2 point, pushing its yield down to 6.40%.
Rates had risen over the last two sessions, fueled by Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan's announcement Friday.
Bonds were helped by a tumble in crude-oil futures.
The second leg of the Treasury's quarterly refunding, where 10year notes were sold, went much as expected.
Thirty-year bonds will be sold today.
"I didn't expect the stock market to do so well today," said Barry Berman, head trader at Robert W. Baird.
"But bonds put on a good performance, and people put money to work."
But Joseph J. McAlinden, chief strategist at Dillon Read, said stocks are enjoying a "normal, technical bounce" from Friday's sharp sell-off.
"The key over the next two days is that if this momentum continues to build, we're probably going to retest highs," Mr. McAlinden said.
He voiced concern that broader market indexes have risen to the same extent as the Dow industrials.
Today, investors will focus on the bond market and the 30-year bond auction.
By tomorrow, all eyes will watch for the release of January producer prices.
The producer price index is an important inflation indicator, and economists are predicting it could be disappointing.
