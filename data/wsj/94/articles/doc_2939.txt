A modest rally in bonds eased investors' fears about rising interest rates, allowing the recovery in stocks to continue.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average jumped 23.92, or 0.62%, to 3856.22.
Broad-based indexes matched that performance, while gainers led losers by a solid 1,443 to 762 on the New York Stock Exchange.
Treasury bonds, which have tumbled recently, sending the yield on the 30-year security to a series of nine-month highs, posted modest gains for a second straight day.
The long bond added more than 3/8 point, nudging its yield down to 6.79%.
Analysts said that was enough to trigger buying in stocks.
Equity prices were battered nearly all of February, and that left many stocks at what some investors think were bargain prices.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index increased 2.17, or 0.47%, to 466.91; the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index rose 1.37, or 0.53%, to 259.07, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index gained 2.19, or 0.50%, to 442.44.
The Nasdaq Composite Index climbed 4.50, or 0.57%, to 795.05.
Technology and health-care stocks, both heavily weighted in that index, were strong again.
The index is about five points, or 0.7%, below its record.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index, meanwhile, added 2.58, or 0.55%, to 469.06.
News of a $1.9 billion merger of Grumman and Martin Marietta also was a psychological positive for stock market investors, said Bill Allyn, managing director and head of listed stock trading at Jefferies & Co.
Aerospace-related and defense-related shares benefited most.
Grumman rocketed 14 1/4, or 36%, to 54 1/8 on volume of two million shares.
Martin Marietta plans to begin a $55-a-share tender offer for the company's stock today.
Martin Marietta shares added 1 1/4 to 46.
Elsewhere in the group, Northrop jumped 1 3/4 to 41 3/8, E-Systems gained 1 3/8 to 44 7/8 and Boeing rose 5/8 to 47 3/4.
General Dynamics, though, lost 2 1/8 to 92 3/8.
Shares of that company climbed 2 7/8 Friday after it declared a 2-for-1 stock split and increased its quarterly dividend.
Economically sensitive stocks were among the strongest again.
They have been the leaders all year as investors bet that recent signs of economic growth will buoy profits.
Big gainers included Caterpillar, which rose 2 to 114 1/8; AlliedSignal, 1 1/8 to 78 1/2, and Deere, 2 7/8 to 89 7/8.
Shares of many consumer-products companies also were strong, especially pharmaceutical companies.
Steven Kroll, a managing director at Monness Crespi & Hardt, said drug stocks gained as investors speculated that President Clinton will have a difficult time getting his health-care overhaul plans enacted, particularly amid growing attention in Washington on the Whitewater affair.
Merck rose 1 to 32, Pfizer jumped 7/8 to 56 7/8, Bristol-Myers Squibb increased 1/2 to 54 5/8 and American Home Products added 3/8 to 59 3/4.
Philip Morris gained 2 to 56 7/8, partly on speculation that a planned increase in the excise tax on cigarettes won't be as large as had been feared.
The tax boost is linked to Mr. Clinton's health-care proposal.
Among other tobacco companies, American Brands added 7/8 to 32 3/4 but RJR Nabisco slipped 1/8 to 6 3/4.
Salomon rose 1 3/4 to 51 3/8 on news that billionaire investor Warren Buffett has spent $272.5 million to increase his stake in the securities company to 19%.
He had held a 14.7% interest.
The additional shares were purchased in February and early this month.
Unum rose 2 3/4 to 55, while Ethyl slipped 3/8 to 17 1/2 .
Shares of Unum, an insurer, will replace those of Ethyl in the S&P 500 index.
Issues added to the index often rise as they are purchased by fund managers whose portfolios are designed to mimic the performance of that market barometer.
Shawmut National lost 3/8 to 20 1/8, Bank of New York fell 3/8 to 51 3/4, BankAmerica declined 5/8 to 41 1/2 and NationsBank slipped 1 1/8 to 46 5/8, all on heavy volume.
Traders said hedge fund manager Michael Steinhardt appeared to be behind some of the selling.
Mr. Steinhardt, who is among big investors said to have been stung by large trading losses recently, couldn't be reached for comment.
USAir tumbled 1 1/4 to 9 after British Airways said it will delay making further investments in the Arlington, Va., airline until USAir's financial conditions improve.
USAir said it expects to report a pretax loss this year that is wider than the $350 million deficit it had in 1993.
British Airways slipped 3/4 to 63 1/2.
Stop & Shop increased 2 1/2 to 27 3/8 after the Boston supermarket company reported fourth-quarter earnings of 36 cents a share, up from 30 cents a year ago.
The mean estimate of analysts surveyed by Zacks Investment Research was 30 cents for the latest period.
American depositary receipts of Bufete Industrial slumped 3 to 42 1/2.
Although the Mexican engineering and construction company's earnings surged last year, sales just edged higher.
"This yesterday's market rise is simply a continuation of the rally that started last Wednesday," said Peter Canelo, a market strategist at NatWest Securities.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average tumbled 50 points intraday that session, only to bounce back by the close.
At its worst that day, the average was 223 points, or 5.6%, below the all-time high it set Jan. 31.
Mr. Canelo said he thinks the correction might be over.
"The market never really should have gone down quite so much.
It's just getting back to levels that are more reasonable, between 3900 and 4000" on the industrial average, he said.
