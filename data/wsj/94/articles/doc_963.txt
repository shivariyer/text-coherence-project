Tokyo stocks slumped 1.4% Wednesday, pummeled by arbitrage selling, with the Nikkei 225-stock index sinking below 20000 for the first time in two weeks.
London shares also sank 1.4%, on disappointing inflation news.
Frankfurt prices rose as German bonds were boosted by the U.S. Federal Reserve's interest-rate tightening and a rate cut by the German central bank.
World-wide, stock prices fell in dollar terms.
The Dow Jones World Stock Index was at 114.95, down 0.09, reflecting lower Asia/Pacific markets and higher American and European markets.
Plantations stocks were the top gainers in the Dow Jones World Industry Groups, closing at 205.77, up 11.03, or 5.7%, with Asiatic of Malaysia posting an 8.4% gain in its local currency.
Oil drilling stocks trailed at 114.18, down 4.33, or 3.7%, with Global Marine of the U.S. sliding 8.1% in local currency.
In Tokyo, the benchmark Nikkei index, which lost 215.92 points on Tuesday, sank 291.43 to 19962.10, the fourth straight session of declines.
In trading Thursday, the Nikkei rose 86.20 points to close the morning session at 20048.30.
Wednesday's first-section volume was estimated at 400 million shares, compared with 370.73 million shares a day earlier.
Losers trounced gainers, 758-274.
The Tokyo Stock Price Index, or Topix, of all first-section issues, which lost 19.23 points Tuesday, skidded 13.59 to 1614.42.
The Nikkei reached its session high of 20322.03 shortly after the opening in reaction to the U.S. Federal Reserve's move to increase the target for the federal-funds rate -- the rate banks charge each other on overnight loans -- by 0.25 percentage point, effectively raising short-term interest rates only slightly.
But selling pressure mounted as corporate and institutional investors dumped issues in an effort to dress up earnings before closing books on Friday, the final trading day for settlement in this fiscal year.
Weakness in futures prices prompted index-linked arbitrage selling late in the day, knocking the Nikkei under 20000.
In London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index slid 46.2 points to 3155.3, near its intraday low.
The FT 30-stock index tumbled 33.8 points, or 1.3%, to 2493.9.
Volume was 714.9 million shares, compared with 678.3 million shares a day earlier.
Stocks opened higher as investors expressed relief that U.S. interest rates rose less than expected.
Later in the day, data showing British inflation in February slowed less than expected drove government bonds and stock-index futures lower, which in turn dragged down the equities market.
The data showed that the annual rate of retail price inflation was 2.4% in February, off from 2.5% in January but above the 2.2% rate economists were expecting.
In Frankfurt, the DAX 30-stock index rose 19.79 points to 2161.13.
Prices stayed in a narrow band amid light trading.
Investors were relieved by the slight increase in U.S. rates.
Participants also were buoyed by encouraging German inflation news and the German central bank's easing of its rate for securities repurchase agreements by 0.25 percentage point to 3.5%.
The German state of North Rhine-Westphalia reported a 3.1% year-on-year consumer price increase, the lowest inflation rate in nearly three years.
In Paris, shares were narrowly mixed in lackluster trading; investors lacked strong incentives for buying.
In Amsterdam, stocks were mixed as the market failed to sustain early strength fueled by a moderate recovery by Dutch bonds.
In Milan, equities slipped slightly as investors remained cautious ahead of Italian elections.
In Madrid, prices rose strongly in choppy trading; sentiment improved after the modest increase in U.S. rates dispelled some uncertainties.
In Stockholm, shares slipped in thin trading, despite lower bond yields and advances for several top blue chips.
In Zurich, stocks advanced 1.1% as investors sought bargains among banking and insurance issues, which had been sold off amid recent interest-rate worries; the small U.S. rate increase helped quell uncertainty.
In Sydney, shares climbed 1.9% after U.S. rates rose only slightly, reducing investor uncertainty.
The Australian government has said domestic interest rates won't rise soon and that inflation is under control.
Foreign investors were bullish about Australian stocks for the first time in weeks.
In Hong Kong, stocks surged 5% as investors put aside concerns about the British colony's interest rates, which are widely expected to rise on Friday for the first time in nearly three years.
The rally came on the heaviest turnover in several weeks and in the face of news that Jardine Matheson Holdings, one of the Hong Kong market's biggest companies, would delist at year end.
Foreign and Hong Kong institutional funds maintained Tuesday's bullishness as they chased blue-chip issues.
In Singapore, stocks jumped 3.2% as worries about a big jump in U.S. interest rates were assuaged.
In Kuala Lumpur, prices surged 3.4% in moderate trading, continuing the rebound that began Tuesday.
In Seoul, equities rose on expectations that South Korea would ease stock trading restrictions, but the gains were damped by rising tensions about North Korea's nuclear-weapons program.
In Taipei, shares rose 1.3% as investors expressed relief at the small rise in U.S. rates.
In Manila, stocks climbed 1.5% in light volume, boosted by a technical rally.
In Wellington, prices edged higher, as bargain-hunting by foreign investors was offset by late weakness in the New Zealand bond market that spurred a sell-off in stocks.
In Bangkok, equities surged 4% as investors continued to gain confidence.
In Mexico City, stocks surged 1.2%, as momentum continued from Tuesday's 5.4% jump on news that Chiapas peace negotiator Manuel Camacho Solis would forgo a presidential bid.
Among corporate developments, in London, Glaxo Holdings tumbled 39 1/2 pence, or 6%, to 623 pence ($9.27).
The drug maker faced mounting concerns about commercial threats to its flagship anti-ulcer drug Zantac.
Lasmo fell 4 pence, or 3.1%, to 124 pence.
The energy group narrowed its 1993 loss, after taxes and exceptional items, to #131 million ($194.9 million) from #392 million a year earlier.
