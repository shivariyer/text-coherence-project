Stocks rallied to a record as investors' fears about rising interest rates continued to ease.
Bond prices jumped for a second straight day, and utilities posted their biggest gain in three years.
That, combined with solid government reports on durable goods orders and jobless claims, sent investors scurrying into a broad range of stocks.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed 18.30, or 0.47%, to 3926.30 -- topping a record that was set last Friday.
Broad-based indexes also jumped to new highs, while advancing issues led decliners 1,329 to 784 on the New York Stock Exchange.
Big Board volume swelled to about 346.3 million shares, up from 304.6 million shares Wednesday and set the highest one-day tally since 363.4 million shares traded Jan. 6.
The Dow Jones Utilities Average rocketed 4.15, or 1.87%, to 225.61, its largest gain since Jan. 17, 1991, when it advanced that same amount.
It hasn't jumped more than that since 1984.
The rally yesterday helped allay concerns about recent losses in that average, which many people view as a leading indicator for stocks and bonds.
The 30-year Treasury bond, meanwhile, rallied 5/8 point, pushing its yield down to 6.26%.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index increased 3.85, or 0.81%, to 477.05; and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 2.12, or 0.81%, to 264.38-both setting records.
The Nasdaq Composite Index advanced 4.08, or 0.52%, to 792.88, while the American Stock Exchange Market Value Index added just 0.12, or 0.02%, to 481.86.
Both are well within 1% of their all-time highs.
The Dow Jones industrials would have leapt even further if not for a steep sell-off in shares of Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing.
That stock tumbled 3 3/4 to 107 1/4 and sliced more than nine points off the Dow.
The company in St. Paul, Minn., reported fourth-quarter earnings of $1.33 a share, up from $1.32 a share a year earlier, but short of the $1.36 mean estimate of analysts surveyed by the Institutional Brokers Estimate System.
Salomon rallied 2 1/4 to 48 1/4 after the New York securities firm said its fourthquarter earnings surged to $4.33 a share from $1.10 a share a year ago.
The company posted a huge profit from its proprietary trading operations.
Sara Lee lost 1 1/2 to 23 1/2 even though the Chicago food concern had a profit of 48 cents a share in the second quarter ended Jan. 1, up from 44 cents a share a year earlier, and in line with analysts' expectations.
U.S. Surgical increased 2 to 29 3/4, a move some analysts tied to speculation the Norwalk, Conn., medical-products company will win a patent infringement lawsuit against Johnson & Johnson.
Shares of the company in New Brunswick, N.J., added 1/2 to 42 3/8.
American depositary receipts of Telefonos de Mexico jumped 2 1/2 to 71 3/8.
The stock had lost ground earlier in the week on concerns that it could face competition in coming years after the Mexican long-distance telephone market is opened to potential rivals.
Compania de Telefonos de Chile's ADRs gained 3 1/4 to 118 1/2.
Morgan Stanley made positive comments about the stock, in part saying it expects the Chilean Congress to give the company permission to expand its operations.
Healthsource jumped 2 7/8 to 57 3/8.
The health-maintenance organization manager in Concord, N.H., declared a 2-for-1 stock split payable March 14 to holders of record Feb. 14.
An offering of 15 million American Depositary shares of Daimler-Benz AG was priced at $46.75 each through underwriters led by C.J. Lawrence/Deutsche Bank, Merrill Lynch and Goldman Sachs.
The shares were sold by Deutsche Bank which announced in September that it planned to sell "roughly" 3% of Daimler's share capital after the Oct. 5 listing of Daimler shares.
Dial increased 1 1/2 to 43 after the personal-care products company in Phoenix reported a fourth-quarter profit of 67 cents a share.
A year-ago, it had income from continuing operations of around 50 cents.
Coca-Cola edged up 3/4 to 41 3/4.
The soft-drink maker in Atlanta, reported that fourth-quarter earnings jumped to 36 cents a share from 30 cents a share a year ago.
McDonald's gained 5/8 to 60.
Fourth-quarter earnings at the fast-food restaurant company were 72 cents a share, up from 61 cents a share a year earlier.
McDonald's sales surged over 7% to $6.15 billion in the quarter.
Ingersoll-Rand the machinery supplier in Woodcliff Lake, N.J., rose 2 1/4 to 39 1/2 on upbeat earnings.
Net income was 65 cents a share in the fourth quarter, up from 31 cents a share a year earlier, when the company recorded a $70 million restructuring charge.
Analysts had been projecting Ingersoll-Rand's earnings at 59 cents a share.
"The market right now seems to be running on all cylinders.
We are seeing remarkable resiliency, even at these high levels," said James Solloway, director of research at Argus Research.
Prices fell Monday and Tuesday on fears that stocks had run too far too fast in the first several weeks of the year.
Michael Metz, a market strategist at Oppenheimer & Co., downplayed the significance of the rally, attributing the gains more to so-called momentum players making bets on the short-term direction of stocks rather than to long-term investors placing wagers based on fundamentals.
"I think it's the very last hurrah before the market turns down.
I would sell into strength rather than follow it," he said.
