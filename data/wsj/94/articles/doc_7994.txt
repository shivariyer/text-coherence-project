Small-company stock prices deteriorated for a second day, but market watchers said it was normal following the strong run-up this year.
The Russell 2000 Index declined 0.84 point, or 0.32%, to 262.05 after falling 0.91 point Monday.
The index, which tracks the performance of small-capitalization stocks traded on the Nasdaq Stock Market and the major exchanges, had set eight straight records before Monday's slide.
A sharp fall in the price of International Business Machines shares during the day pushed down some small technology stocks, and they never recovered.
Among the affected small tech stocks were Peak Technologies Group, a New York distributor of bar-code data-collection equipment, which slid 7/8 to 9 1/8; Radius, a San Jose, Calif., visual computing systems concern, down 1 to 7 3/8; and Weitek, a Sunnyvale, Calif., designer of high-performance numeric-processing circuits and software, down 7/8 to 9.
But Exar jumped 1 1/2 to 23 1/4 on fiscal third-quarter earnings of 56 cents a share, up from 51 cents a year earlier.
The San Jose, Calif., company designs, manufactures and markets analog and mixed-signal, application-specific integrated circuits.
Good earnings also helped Signal Technology climb 5/8 to 5 1/2.
The Weymount, Mass., maker of electronic components and subsystems reported fourth-quarter net income of 18 cents a share on more shares, compared with 11 cents a year earlier.
LanOptics, Israel, skidded 3 7/8 to 13 5/8.
Late Monday, the developer and manufacturer of wiring concentrators and related network-management systems said preliminary fourth-quarter results indicate net will be about $600,000, or 10 cents a share, compared with $1 million, or 21 cents a share, a year earlier.
BroadBand Technologies, Durham, N.C., surged 2 to 28 after Goldman, Sachs & Co. initiated coverage of the stock with a "buy" rating and added the telecommunications company to its recommended list.
Interest-rate-sensitive stocks fell as bonds slid.
The Treasury's 30-year bond fell more than 1/2 point, pushing its yield up to 6.34%.
Anchor Bancorp Wisconsin, Madison, Wis., fell 1/2 to 23 1/4; Bank of New Hampshire, Manchester, N.H., declined 1/2 to 18; and Provident Bancorp, Cincinnati, gave up 1/2 to 31.
Elsewhere, WLR Foods rocketed 9 1/2 to 28 1/2 .
Late Monday, Tyson Foods said it has proposed to buy WLR, a Hinton, Va., producer of poultry and meat products, for $30 a share in cash.
Yesterday, WLR Food President and Chief Executive Officer James Keeler said Tyson's proposal was "totally unsolicited, unwanted and out of line with WLR Foods' long-term business plans and corporate philosophy."
On the Border Cafes, a Dallas-based owner and operator of casual restaurants, gained 1 1/8 to 8 3/8.
Brinker International (NYSE), a restaurant concern, said it has entered into a definitive merger agreement to acquire On the Border Cafes.
The weak financial and technology issues caused the Nasdaq Composite Index to lose 4.26 points, or 0.54%, to end at 786.39.
"It was just time to take a pause," said William Sulya, head of over-the-counter trading at A.G. Edwards & Sons Inc.
"Nothing goes straight up, as the old saying goes."
Steven Goldman, a market strategist at Weeden & Co., said investors took profits because "things had gotten pretty stretched, and there was too much enthusiasm."
Losing Nasdaq issues ran ahead of gainers, 1,690 to 1,379.
Nasdaq National Market volume totaled 297,149,600 shares, while overall volume stood at 339,203,700 shares.
Monday, National Market volume was 258,388,600 shares, and total volume was 303,884,000.
Nasdaq National Market stocks that set highs for the year to date totaled 67, while 25 hit lows.
Observers said more profit-taking might occur in the next few sessions, although it "doesn't indicate that things are coming unglued," Mr. Sulya said.
"It's just a normal correction in a healthy bull market."
Since 1994 began, the Russell 2000 is up 3.46 points, or 1.34%, and the Nasdaq Composite has risen 9.8 points, or 1.26%, even accounting for the losses posted in the past two sessions.
