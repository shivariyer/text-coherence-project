A steep sell-off in bonds depressed stocks, but strength in some cyclicals partially offset the gloom.
Although the Treasury's 30-year bond skidded almost a point -- lifting its yield to another nine-month high -- the Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped a benign 4.50, or 0.12%, to 3851.72.
Broad-based indexes, which are less heavily weighted with economically sensitive stocks, fell somewhat further than the Dow.
Declining issues outpaced advancers by 1,174 to 944 on the New York Stock Exchange.
Volume on the exchange was active at 298,039,000 shares, up from 285,571,000 Monday.
The Labor Department gave a solid reading on fourth-quarter business productivity yesterday morning, while Lynch Jones Ryan's Johnson Redbook affiliate released improved retail sales data in the afternoon.
Economically sensitive gainers included International Paper, which jumped 1 7/8 to 74 1/4; International Business Machines, 2 1/2 to 54 3/4; and Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing, 1/2 to 104 7/8.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index lost 1.03, or 0.22%, to 465.88 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index slipped 0.59, or 0.23%, to 258.48.
The Nasdaq Composite Index lost 2.93, or 0.37%, to 792.12.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index declined 1.38, or 0.29%, to 467.68.
Hugh Johnson, chief investment officer at First Albany, said the weakness in bonds hit many interest-rate-sensitive stocks hard, especially insurance and utilities shares.
American International lost 1 7/8 to 82 7/8 and St. Paul Cos. slipped 1 1/8 to 79.
The Dow Jones Utilities Average tumbled 1.85, or 0.87%, to 211.64.
Banking and securities brokerage issues, the other major financial groups, though, finished mixed.
BankAmerica lost 3/8 to 41 1/8 on the Big Board and Michigan National fell 1/4 to 64 1/2 on the Nasdaq market, while NationsBank added 3/8 to 47 and Bank of New York rose 1/4 to 52.
Merrill Lynch fell 5/8 to 40 5/8 but Charles Schwab jumped 1 7/8 to 28 1/2.
General Motors added 1/2 to 62 after Goldman Sachs added the stock to its recommended list.
Chrysler added 3/8 to 59 7/8, but Ford Motor slipped 5/8 to 63 3/4.
Varity climbed 4 3/8 to 48 7/8.
The auto parts maker had a fourth-quarter profit of 59 cents a fully diluted share, up from 23 cents before a charge a year earlier.
Compaq Computer jumped 2 1/4 to 100 1/2, aided by positive comments from Kidder Peabody.
Analyst David Korus said the prospects are good for better-than-expected revenue growth at the personal-computer maker.
Eli Lilly rose 3/4 to 53 1/4 after the drug maker signed a letter of intent to acquire Sphinx Pharmaceuticals for $6 a share, or about $80 million.
Analysts said the move should boost Lilly's research effort.
Shares of Sphinx jumped 2 9/16 to 5 7/16 on Nasdaq.
SynOptics Communications slipped 1/2 to 24 on Nasdaq.
Observers said analysts at PaineWebber and Goldman Sachs both made negative comments about the maker of computer networking products.
Dayton Hudson jumped 2 1/2 to 75 3/4.
A Lehman Brothers analyst upgraded a rating on the stock to "outperform."
The Minneapolis retailer's stock had been rated "neutral."
Burlington Industries increased 1 to 17.
The textile company said the Labor Department won't take any action following completion of an investigation of Burlington's employee stock ownership plan.
American depositary receipts of Mexican companies slid as the Mexican Stock Market tumbled more than 1%.
Telefonos de Mexico topped the Big Board's most-active list, losing 1 7/8 to 67.
Grupo Tribasa, a construction issue, dropped 7/8 to 33 3/8.
Coca-Cola Femsa, a beverage stock, declined 1 3/4 to 30 1/8.
Syntex advanced 3/4 to 15 1/2 after it won approval from the Food and Drug Administration to market its Cattlyst product, a feed additive designed to promote growth in beef cattle.
MGM Grand skidded 1 7/8 to 29 3/8.
Smith Barney Shearson trimmed its estimate of the Las Vegas casino operator's first-quarter earnings to 15 cents a share from a previous forecast of 35 cents.
Smith Barney, though, still rates the stock a "buy."
Westinghouse slipped 1/4 to 13 7/8.
The company said it expects to report first-quarter earnings of 10 cents a share, down from 14 cents in the year-ago period.
It cited higher pension costs and lower licensee income.
Many oil-related stocks declined.
Atlantic Richfield was down 2 1/8 to 97 7/8, Mobil slid 3/4 to 77 3/4, Anadarko Petroleum lost 2 1/4 to 43 3/8 and Unocal 3/8 to 26 3/4.
Schlumberger, an oil field services provider, eased 3/8 to 56 1/8.
Timothy Heekin, head of block trading at Salomon Brothers, said some of the good news that hurt bonds yesterday helped stocks, particularly the cyclicals.
"We are still seeing a very strong economy, and that is keeping people interested in the stock market," he said.
