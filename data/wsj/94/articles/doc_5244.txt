Relieved by a partial recovery in the value of the U.S. dollar, investors bid stock prices solidly higher.
Steep losses in the dollar against the Japanese yen erased a midday rally Monday.
But yesterday the stock-market buying resumed as the U.S. currency retook nearly one-fourth of the ground lost a day earlier.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average jumped 24.21, or 0.62%, to 3,928.27.
Economically sensitive components led the advance, including AlliedSignal, which rose 1 3/4 to 79 3/4, and Goodyear Tire, which jumped 1 1/2 to 47 1/2.
Broad-based indexes also advanced, and the Nasdaq Stock Market recorded its second-straight day of solid gains.
On the New York Stock Exchange, advancing issues led decliners 1,198 to 882.
Volume on the exchange jumped to about 306.7 million shares from 263.1 million Monday.
The price of the benchmark 30-year U.S. Treasury bond fell 1/32 to 97 8/32, to yield 6.45%, unchanged from Monday.
The search for value helped lift many consumer-products shares, such as food issues, said Edward Nicoski, a market analyst at Piper Jaffray.
Kellogg jumped 1 3/4 to 53 3/8, Hershey rallied 2 7/8 to 51 1/2 and Ralston-Purina added 1 to 44 1/8.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index increased 2.29, or 0.49%, to 472.52 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index added 1.10, or 0.42%, to 262.36.
The Nasdaq Composite Index, which rose 4.06 Monday, was up 4.67, or 0.59%, to 790.12, aided by technology and healthcare issues.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index was the laggard for a third-straight session, easing 0.07, or 0.01%, to 474.84.
Shares of Motorola rose 2 1/8 to 101 3/8 as U.S. Trade Representative Mickey Kantor confirmed that U.S. is planning trade sanctions against Japan if that country doesn't live up to an agreement to open its markets further for the Schaumburg, Ill., company's cellular telephones.
The dispute over cellular phones is just part of the trade tensions between the U.S. and Japan that have disrupted financial markets since Friday.
Viacom's Class B stock dropped 1 7/8 to 28 on the Amex and QVC Network added 1 3/4 to 50 1/4 after QVC conceded defeat in the takeover battle for Paramount Communications.
Shares of the New York media company rose 7/8 to 77 on the Big Board.
BellSouth, which backed QVC, added 1/8 to 56 1/8 , while Blockbuster Entertainment, which also will merge with Viacom, added 3/8 to 25 1/4.
Elf Aquitaine's American depositary receipts jumped 1 1/8 to 36 3/4 on heavy volume of 4.7 million shares.
Demand was high for a public offering of the oil and gas company's shares that was priced late Monday.
The issue was sold by the French government.
National Semiconductor rallied 1 3/4 to 20 5/8.
PaineWebber upgraded a rating on the Santa Clara, Calif., chipmaker's stock to "buy" from "attractive."
Other chip shares jumped.
Micron Technology gained 2 7/8 to 61 1/2, Texas Instruments rose 2 7/8 to 76 3/8 and Intel increased 1 1/8 to 65 on the Nasdaq Stock Market.
Motorola, meanwhile, also is a big player in the semiconductor industry.
United HealthCare slipped 5/8 to 85 after the Minnetonka, Minn., company agreed to acquire Ramsay-HMO through a stock swap valued at $500 million.
Shares of Coral Gables, Fla., Ramsay-HMO rocketed 9 3/8, or 20.4%, to 55 1/4.
Other health-maintenance-organization stocks advanced.
Oxford Health Plans of Norwalk, Conn., jumped 3 1/4 to 65 1/4 ; U.S. Healthcare of Blue Bell, Pa., increased 3 1/4 to 63 3/4 and FHP International of Fountain Valley, Calif., rose 1 5/8 to 28 5/8.
Each trades on Nasdaq.
Atlantic Richfield shed 3 1/4 to 104 3/8.
Dean Witter Reynolds analyst Eugene Nowak cut his estimate for the Los Angeles oil and gas company's 1994 operating income, citing continued weakness in oil prices.
Crude-oil futures for March delivery briefly moved under $14 a barrel in New York yesterday before ending little changed.
Scientific-Atlanta increased 2 to 29.
The Norcross, Ga., company agreed to develop multimedia products jointly with Oracle Systems.
Separately, Salomon Brothers raised Scientific-Atlanta shares to "buy" from "neutral."
Oracle's stock inched up 7/8 to 33 5/8 on Nasdaq.
Salomon cut SynOptics Communications, a Santa Clara, Calif., networking products company, to "hold" from "buy."
The stock lost 1 3/8 to 26 1/8 on Nasdaq.
CML Group increased 1 1/2 to 21 1/4.
CNBC cable television reported that investment manager Martin Sands, of Sands Bros. & Co., expects strong earnings from the Acton, Mass., maker of the NordicTrack exercise machine.
That, Sands said, could trigger a so-called short squeeze and sharp gains in the stock, of which Sands Bros. is a big holder.
Reliance Group Holdings slipped 3/4 to 6.
Several analysts trimmed their 1994 earnings forecasts for the New York insurance company after it reported 1993 results yesterday.
"Rather than being a headache", like they were Monday, "the dollar and the interest rate markets were benign" yesterday, said Greg Nie, a technical analyst at Kemper Securities.
That encouraged investors to buy some of the stocks they felt were still undervalued after the market's steep plunge on Feb. 4, he said.
"The fact that we are getting bottom-fishing in some of these depressed areas would indicate that there is still an abundance of money out there looking for a place," said Piper Jaffray's Mr. Nicoski.
That's a positive sign for the market, although he said a modest correction might still be in the offing.
