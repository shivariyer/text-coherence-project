A recovery in the bond market cleared the way for another record-setting day on Wall Street.
After a one-day slide, the Dow Jones Industrial Average bounced back, gaining 11.53, or 0.29%, to 3975.54 -- less than three points from its all-time high of 3978.36 and back within striking distance of 4,000.
Broad-based indexes were even stronger, setting highs in many cases.
Advancing issues outpaced decliners about 1,279 to 851 on the New York Stock Exchange.
Volume there climbed to about 328,770,000 shares from 322,440,000 Tuesday.
The average price per share rose 17 cents.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index increased 2.38, or 0.50%, to 482.00; the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index rose 1.27, or 0.48%, to 267.71, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index added 2.07, or 0.46%, to 456.27.
All three closed at record levels.
The Nasdaq Composite Index climbed 2.33, or 0.29%, to 799.57 -- less than a point from its all-time high.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index advanced 1.90, or 0.39%, to 487.89 -- a third straight record.
The Treasury's 30-year bond retook more than 1/2 point of the full point it lost Tuesday.
The yield on the long bond eased back to 6.28%.
Economically sensitive stocks, among the strongest market segments for weeks, were joined in the leadership position yesterday by oil-related issues.
Big gainers included Union Carbide, which rose 1 1/8 to 25 3/4; Texaco, which rallied 1 1/4 to 67 3/4, and Baker Hughes, which jumped 1 1/4 to 21 1/4.
Sears Roebuck tumbled 2 1/2 to 51 1/4, as 6.9 million of its shares changed hands, leading Big Board volume.
Prudential Securities downgraded the big Chicago retailer to "hold" from "buy."
Amgen (Nasdaq) slumped 3 to 44 after the company told analysts that sales growth for its Neupogen drug likely will ease this year.
Rumors about a slowdown have battered the stock all week.
The Thousand Oaks, Calif., company's stock ended at 49 3/4 on Friday.
The drug is used to control infections in patients undergoing cancer chemotherapy.
Paramount Communications lost 1 7/8 to 78.
Some investors were said to be disappointed that Viacom and QVC Network weren't more aggressive late Tuesday when they sweetened their bids for the New York media company.
Although the two offers carry roughly the same value, investors yesterday gave Viacom an edge in the takeover battle, citing provisions in its proposal that protect investors from a potential slide in its stock price.
Viacom's Class B stock (AMEX) lost 5/8 to 33 1/2.
QVC (Nasdaq) added 2 to 46 3/4.
Blockbuster Entertainment, which is backing Viacom, lost 3/4 to 26 7/8.
BellSouth, which is aiding QVC, dropped 3/4 to 59.
Polaroid dropped 1 3/8 to 33 5/8.
Although the Cambridge, Mass., camera maker reported strong fourth-quarter earnings Tuesday, analysts yesterday cautioned that the company's earnings could be depressed early this year.
Empresas la Moderna jumped to 35 3/4 in its first day of trading on volume of 4.2 million shares.
The big Mexican cigarette producer had an initial offering of 5.3 million American depositary shares, priced at $32.53 apiece.
Sprint added 1 5/8 to 37 1/8.
The Kansas City, Mo., telephone service provider reported fourth-quarter earnings of 55 cents a share, compared with income from operations of 34 cents a year earlier.
General Electric shares increased 2 1/4 to 109 1/8.
Officials of the diversified Fairfield, Conn., company couldn't account for the rise.
Shares of precious metals producers eased after surging Tuesday.
American Barrick fell 1 to 27 5/8, Homestake Mining dropped 1 3/8 to 21 7/8, Newmont Mining declined 1 to 54 1/2 and Echo Bay Mines (AMEX) slipped 3/8 to 13 3/8.
USF&G rose 1 1/2 to 15 3/8.
The Baltimore insurance company reported fourth-quarter earnings of 55 cents a share, up from 1 cent a year earlier.
The latest period included a gain from a tax benefit.
Flightsafety climbed 4 1/2 to 39 1/4.
The New York company, which provides training to pilots, had a profit of 61 cents a share in the fourth quarter, up from 52 cents a year earlier.
Analysts said investors, in part, were encouraged by the market's relatively minor losses in the face of bad news Tuesday, including the bond slump and a jump in commodities prices.
The Dow was off just 14.35 yesterday after surging 70 points in the previous three trading days.
"I think investors came in with some confidence yesterday that the real trend in stocks is up," said Richard Scarlatta, a newsletter editor at Sutton Financial Services in Chicago.
Many big investors still have money to spend, said Ricky Harrington, a technical analyst at Interstate-Johnson Lane.
Inflows to equity mutual funds were at a record level in December, and fund executives have projected perhaps even larger inflows for January.
