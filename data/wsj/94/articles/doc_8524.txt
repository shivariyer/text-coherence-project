Investors got bullish in the final minutes of trading and pushed the stock market up as the Dow Jones Industrial Average moved further into record territory.
Stock prices had been mixed for most of the day, but observers said continued optimism about earnings reports, strong bonds and good economic data ignited across-the-board buying right before the close.
The Dow industrials finished 7.59 points, or 0.20%, higher at 3,891.96, its second-straight record.
The index also finished at a record level Monday and tied that record Tuesday.
Yesterday's all-time high is the eighth record finish this year.
News of a jump in December housing starts boosted cyclical issues.
Bethlehem Steel rose 1/4 to 22 1/2, and AlliedSignal rose 7/8 to 79 1/4.
Meanwhile, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index added 0.28 point to 263.23, its second-consecutive record close.
The Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index gained 0.68 point to end at 474.98.
Both indexes had spent most of the day lower.
Big Board volume totaled 310.4 million shares, compared with 310.8 million shares Wednesday.
Advancing issues led declining by 1,110 to 960.
The 30-year Treasury bond was hit early on by the encouraging economic data but quickly reversed course, and gained 3/8 point.
Its yield fell to 6.26%.
With the Dow industrials testing new heights every day, analysts suggested 3,900 and perhaps 4,000 on the blue chip average may be within reach over the next few weeks.
Tomorrow's "double witching" session could make for a little volatility, some observers said, though they aren't expecting any fireworks.
Options on individual stocks and some stock indexes are due to expire.
The Nasdaq Composite Index recouped what it lost Wednesday-rising 3.74 points to end at a record 793.02.
Technology stocks powered the move during both sessions.
Nasdaq-traded Intel of Santa Clara, Calif., rose 1 1/4 to 63 3/4, after stumbling 4 3/4 Wednesday following earnings results that were below expectations.
Alex. Brown & Sons Inc. lowered its rating on the chip maker to "buy" from "strong buy" yesterday morning.
Microsoft rose 3 to 86 1/4 in Nasdaq trading on improved fiscal second-quarter net income reported late Wednesday of 95 cents a share, up from 78 cents a year earlier.
Several brokerage firms upgraded the Redmond, Wash., company's shares, though Cowen & Co. lowered its recommendation to "neutral" from "buy."
The American Stock Exchange Market Value index gained 1.53 to 484.81, an all-time high.
Tandem Computers moved 1 5/8 higher to 13 3/4 on a good earnings report.
Apple Computer, Cupertino, Calif., gained 5/8 to 29 7/8 on Nasdaq, but afterhours trading on Reuters Holdings' electronic Instinet systems shows the last trade at 31 1/8 on significant volume.
After the market closed, the computer company reported fiscal first-quarter earnings of 34 cents a share, one cent above analysts' estimates, though below the $1.33 earned a year ago.
The computer maker also said operating expenses fell and Macintosh personal-computer sales rose.
But Computer Associates International toppled 3 1/2 to 41.
A 50% jump in fiscal third-quarter earnings reported Wednesday wasn't enough to please investors who cling to memories of the previous quarter's 82% leap, analysts said.
Elsewhere on the earnings front, Kimberly-Clark surged 3 5/8 to 56 1/8.
The consumer products concern said fourth-quarter earnings came in at 88 cents a share, compared with a loss of 35 cents on a charge a year earlier.
Wall Street expectations were for net income of 79 cents during the latest quarter.
Bankers Trust New York gained 1 1/8 to 79.
The company said its fourth-quarter earnings climbed to $3.26 a share from $1.49 a share a year earlier, while analysts had looked for net income of just $2.50.
In addition, Bankers Trust said it expects to buy back one million to two million common shares during the first quarter of 1994.
Bombay declined 2 1/2 to 29 1/2.
The Fort Worth, Texas, operator of home-furnishings retail stores posted fiscal second-quarter earnings of 40 cents a share, which reflect a 2-for-1 stock split paid Dec. 31, 1993.
Analysts had looked for the company to earn 39 cents a share.
Among new issues, Plantronics zoomed to 15, above its initial offering price of $12.50 a share late Wednesday.
Plantronics, a designer and maker of lightweight telephone headsets, said it will use proceeds to fund a recapitalization plan that will reduce the company's debt and interest expenses.
American depositary receipts of some Mexican stocks rose.
Telefonos de Mexico climbed 1 5/8 to 69 3/8, after it said it will expand its line network 12%, or about 900,000 lines this year, to 8.3 million lines.
Others gained ground as investors dismissed expected rebel activity in Mexico as a threat to Latin American markets.
Vitro added 1 3/8 to 24, Grupo Financiero Serfin increased 2 1/8 to 32 1/2, Grupo Tribasa gained 1 1/4 to 34 and Empresas ICA Sociedad Controladora jumped 2 to 30.
Chilean chemical-fertilizer concern Sociedad Quimica Y Minera added 7/8 to 31 7/8, and Argentina's Banco de Galicia edged up 1/2 to 41 1/8 in Nasdaq trading.
But transportation stocks ended weak.
CSX fell 2 3/8 to 86 1/2 on lower fourth-quarter earnings of $1.46 a share, down from $1.47 a year earlier.
Union Pacific dropped 2 1/2 to 63 7/8, despite posting improved fourth-quarter earnings.
Time Warner gained 7/8 to 40.
The media giant said its board has adopted a shareholder-rights plan that "does not preclude a bona fide all-cash offer for all Time Warner's outstanding stock."
Bear Stearns & Co. raised its rating on Time Warner to "strong buy" from "buy."
