Renewed optimism about the economy lifted cyclical stocks and left the market mixed.
With Tuesday's news on interest rates out of the way, investors turned their attention back to signs of life in the economy, and that triggered buying in cyclicals, said James Solloway, director of research at Argus Research.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average climbed 6.91 to 3869.46, aided by big gains in stocks such as Caterpillar, which jumped 2 to 120 3/4, and International Paper, which rose 1 1/4 to 70 3/8.
Broad-based indexes were mixed, but advancing issues outpaced decliners on the New York Stock Exchange by 1,159 to 945.
Volume on the exchange was moderately active at 281,420,000 shares, compared with 282,240,000 Tuesday.
Shares of the Big Three U.S. auto makers were among the beneficiaries of the optimism.
General Motors gained 7/8 to 60 1/2, Ford Motor jumped 1 3/4 to 63 3/8 and Chrysler rose 3/4 to 58 1/8.
Chrysler disclosed that it expects to post near-record sales this month.
Other transportation-related issues were strong.
The Dow Jones Transportation Average was up 10.59 to 1,725.01, aided by Airborne Freight, which jumped 7/8 to 36 1/4, and Roadway Services, which rallied 2 1/2 to 70 3/4 on the Nasdaq Stock Market.
A late retreat in bonds, though, was enough to take away some of the exuberance.
Profit-taking carried stocks well off their highs late in the day as the price of long-term bonds dropped more than 1/2 point and the yield rose to 6.89%.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index finished with a loss of 0.26, or 0.06%, to 468.54 and the New York Stock Exchange Composite added 0.04, or 0.02%, to 260.28.
The Nasdaq Composite Index climbed 1.17, or 0.15%, to 797.51.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index advanced 1.65, or 0.35%, to 473.38.
Drug stocks were weak after Gruntal & Co. cut its investment rating on the group to "neutral" from "outperform," citing concerns about price cutting in the industry.
Pfizer lost 1 1/2 to 55 1/2, Glaxo Holdings' American depositary receipts fell 1 to 18 1/2, Teva Pharmaceutical slumped 2 3/8 to 25 7/8 on Nasdaq and Forest Laboratories dropped 3 3/4 to 47 1/4 on the Amex.
Rubbermaid lost 1 3/8 to 28 3/8 after the company said its first-quarter earnings will fall short of some analysts' expectations.
The Wooster, Ohio, rubber and plastic-products maker forecast earnings of about 32 cents a share, up from 31 cents last year but below analysts' projections of about 35 cents.
Fingerhut climbed 7/8 to 32 1/2.
Bear Stearns & Co. analyst Steven Kernkraut upgraded the stock to "buy."
He had rated shares of the Minnetonka, Minn., direct-mail merchandiser "hold."
International Business Machines slipped 1 to 57 1/4.
The Wall Street Journal's "Heard on the Street" column yesterday reported that executives have little room for error in their efforts to revive the Armonk, N.Y., computer giant.
Restructuring charges have driven IBM's net worth down so far that its long-term debt is now the equivalent of 77% of shareholder equity.
Global Marine lost 3/8 to 4 1/4 after the Houston oil and gas-drilling contractor said it expects to post a loss of three cents to four cents a share for the first quarter.
The company will record a $3.5 million charge in the period for accounting adjustments.
It had a loss of six cents a year earlier.
Tobacco stocks rebounded somewhat after tumbling Tuesday on news that a House of Representatives subcommittee voted to recommend a steeper-than-expected increase in the excise tax on cigarettes.
Philip Morris, the subject of an investment-rating upgrade by Merrill Lynch & Co., added 3/4 to 52 7/8.
RJR Nabisco gained 1/4 to 6 3/8.
Pep Boys rose 1 1/4 to 28 1/4.
The company's earnings jumped to 25 cents a share in the fourth quarter ended Jan. 29 from 20 cents a year ago.
The Philadelphia automotive-parts retailers said its new fiscal year has "gotten off to a solid start."
Colgate-Palmolive lost 2 to 60 5/8 after Bear Stearns & Co. cut a rating on the stock to "hold" from "buy," citing concerns that the New York household and personal-care products maker's operating income won't grow as much as had been expected in the first quarter.
Seagram added 3/4 to 30 7/8.
The Canadian beverage company reported earnings of 47 cents a share for the fourth quarter ended Jan. 31, excluding a charge.
That was up from 40 cents before a charge in the year-ago period.
Novell continued to slide, losing 1 to 19 on Nasdaq-leading volume of 21.8 million shares.
The Provo, Utah, software maker's stock has dropped since it disclosed plans for an acquisition earlier this week.
Several analysts cut investment ratings Tuesday.
Analysts said the Federal Reserve's move Tuesday to just mildly tighten credit relieved some of the recent uncertainty about interest rates.
The central bank's lack of intervention in the money markets yesterday appeared to confirm speculation that short-term rates would rise a slim 0.25 percentage point.
"What people are coming to believe is that the economy is chugging away and the Fed is doing all the right things to make sure that inflation doesn't get out of hand," said Dale Tills, vice president of trading at Charles Schwab & Co.
Meanwhile, there was more potentially disturbing news after the close of trading as several major banks lifted their prime lending rates to 6.25% from 6%.
Don Hays, a market strategist at Wheat First, Butcher & Singer, said that could be a temporary setback for stocks this morning but said it doesn't change his overall bullish outlook.
