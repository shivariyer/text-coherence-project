The Dow Jones Industrial Average plodded to another high as investors continued to funnel cash into economically sensitive stocks.
The Dow industrials climbed 3.09 points to 3870.29, its eighth record this year.
Gains in oil stocks and a handful of industrial stocks propelled the index of 30 blue-chip stocks higher.
Caterpillar jumped 2 to 92 1/2, Goodyear Tire & Rubber 1 7/8 to 49 1/8, Aluminum Co. of America 1 1/4 to 74 1/4 and Union Carbide 1 1/8 to 24 1/4.
But the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index lost 1.61 points to 473.30 and the Nasdaq Composite index eased 0.13 to 792.18, retreating from its record set Friday.
Declining issues outnumbered advancers, 1,071 to 994.
Volume on the New York Stock Exchange dropped to 226.4 million shares, the slowest single day this year, from 305 million shares Friday.
Trading activity was sluggish as many investors were away for the observance of Martin Luther King Jr.'s birthday.
Analysts said that despite the lackluster day, investors were snapping up cyclical stocks.
"With the economy picking up speed and earnings starting to come out, investors are realizing that it's economically sensitive stocks which are going to produce the earnings gains," said Thomas McManus, an analyst at Morgan Stanley.
Mr. McManus noted that since the start of the year, the investment bank's index of cyclical stocks has climbed 3.52% while its barometer of consumer stocks has sunk 1.13%.
That divergence continued yesterday as the cyclical group rose 1.07 points to 310 while the basket of consumer stocks lost 1.25 to 198.03.
Steven Kroll, a managing director at Monness Crespi & Hardt, said an upbeat earnings forecast from Union Carbide helped trigger the gains in the cyclical group.
Union Carbide astonished investors when it said that it expects fourth-quarter 1993 earnings to be about 25 cents a share on a fully diluted basis, up from 12 cents last year and above analysts' expectations.
Meanwhile, farm-machinery stocks rallied after a trade group reported robust gains in December retail sales.
Deere jumped 3 1/8 to 79 5/8 and Tenneco rose 1 3/8 to 56 1/2.
But Coca-Cola slumped 7/8 to 41 5/8.
In an interview in Barron's, renowned money manager John Neff panned the stock, saying that it could be "the Philip Morris of this or maybe next year."
Meanwhile, many investors were preoccupied with reports from Southern California, where a severe earthquake killed at least 20 people and caused extensive damage around Los Angeles.
Shares of property and casualty insurance companies slipped on concerns about the quake's cost.
Safeco eased 1/4 to 53 7/8 on the Nasdaq Stock Market, while on the Big Board, Chubb fell 3/8 to 77 3/8, Allstate slipped 1 to 28 5/8 and CNA Financial declined 1 3/4 to 76 1/4.
Parametric Technology tumbled 5 to 33 1/4 on Nasdaq.
The software company in Waltham, Mass., reported net income of 25 cents a share for the first quarter ended Jan. 1, up from 14 cents last year, and in line with analysts' forecasts.
Some investors, though, were disappointed with its revenue growth.
Qualcomm skidded 3 1/8 to 51 on Nasdaq.
The San Diego wireless communications company reported first-quarter earnings of 18 cents a share, up from a loss of eight cents last year, but short of the 21 cents mean estimate of analysts surveyed by IBES, the Institutional Brokers Estimate System.
Archer-Daniels-Midland increased 1 3/8 to 25 7/8.
The company reported earnings of 44 cents a share for the second quarter ended last month, versus 45 cents a year earlier.
The effect of commodity price increases on inventory valuations cut the latest quarter's income by seven cents a share.
International Business Machines lost 1 1/8 to 57 1/2.
Kemper Securities analyst Joseph Payne downgraded his rating on the Armonk, N.Y., company's stock to "reduce" from "hold."
He said the stock is overvalued based on Big Blue's earnings performance.
Rockwell International jumped 1 1/2 to 39 1/8 after the aerospace company in Seal Beach, Calif., reported first-quarter earnings of 68 cents a share, up from 58 cents a year ago, and above the 62 cents mean estimate reported by IBES.
Caremark jumped 1 1/2 to 21 after the Northbrook, Ill., company agreed to buy Medical Care America's home-infusion unit for $175 million.
Shares of Medical Care in Dallas, eased 7/8 to 23 1/8.
American depositary receipts of Volvo surged 4 1/4 to 81 1/8.
Hedge fund manager George Noble made positive comments about the Swedish auto maker in an interview published in this week's Barron's magazine.
Shares of many large consumer products-oriented growth companies lost ground.
Philip Morris was off 1 to 56 5/8, General Mills fell 1 1/8 to 58 7/8 and Walt Disney eased 1 to 46 5/8.
Precious-metals producers advanced with the price of gold.
American Barrick gained 1/2 to 30 3/4, Homestake Mining climbed 7/8 to 24 7/8, and on the American Stock Exchange, Echo Bay Mines rose 1/2 to 14 7/8.
Gold for February delivery gained $2.10 to $392.20 an ounce in New York.
