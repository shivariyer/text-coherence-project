Concerns about rising interest rates nudged stock prices lower, but the market finished above its worst levels of the day.
After the market's sharp gains in the past several weeks, analysts said investors are becoming more cautious.
Moderate losses in bond prices triggered profit taking yesterday, they said.
The Treasury's 30-year bond was down more than 3/8 point yesterday to yield 6.31%.
Earlier, it was off more than 1/2 point.
The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which rallied to within three points of a record Wednesday, fell 7.88, or 0.20%, to 3967.66.
Broad-based indexes fell about the same.
The industrial average had skidded to a loss of more than 25 points earlier in the day.
Big Board volume topped the active 300 million-share-level for the eighth straight session, at 318,000,000 shares, compared with 328,770,000 Wednesday.
Declining issues led advancers 1,190 to 918.
The average price per share fell seven cents.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index lost 1.29, or 0.27%, to 480.71; the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index fell 0.53, or 0.20%, to 267.18; and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index slipped 1.11, or 0.24%, to 455.16.
All set records Wednesday.
The Nasdaq Composite Index dropped 1.78, or 0.22%, to 797.79.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 0.71, or 0.15%, to 487.18 -- ending a streak of three straight record highs.
The Dow Jones Utilities Average fell twice as far as the Dow industrials, sliding 0.92, or 0.41%, to 224.23.
The focus on interest rates left financial stocks lower.
Citicorp dropped 1 to 42 1/2; Chase Manhattan fell 3/4 to 35 1/4; American International, an insurer, skidded 1 3/8 to 89 1/2; Equitable Cos. fell 1 1/2 to 28; and PaineWebber eased 5/8 to 27 7/8.
Shares of the Big Three auto makers advanced after the release of surprisingly strong sales figures.
U.S. auto sales reached a 7.2 million-unit annual rate in January, topping the 6.7 million rate analysts had expected.
General Motors gained 1 1/2 to 63, Ford Motor rose 1 5/8 to 69 3/8 and Chrysler added 1 1/4 to 62 1/2.
RJR Nabisco climbed 1/4 to 7 7/8 in heavy trading amid rumors that the company planned to spin off its tobacco unit.
RJR, responding to inquiries from the New York Stock Exchange, said that it knows of no reason for the unusual activity in its stock yesterday.
RJR declined to comment on the market rumors.
This spring marks the fifth anniversary of the Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co.-led leveraged buyout of RJR, making KKR eligible for a tax-free spinoff.
Also, in May, RJR is expected to retire more than $2 billion in high-coupon debt that contains restrictive covenants that make a separation of the company difficult.
Maytag slumped 2 1/8 to 17 1/4 after the Newton, Iowa, home-appliance maker reported lower-than-expected fourth-quarter earnings.
It had a profit of 16 cents a share in the period, up from 11 cents a year earlier, but below the 23-cent mean estimate of analysts surveyed by the Institutional Brokers Estimate System.
Whirlpool jumped 1 1/4 to 72 3/8.
Excluding charges, the Benton Harbor, Mich., appliance maker's net income climbed to $1.13 a share in the fourth quarter from $1.02 a year earlier.
Many retailing stocks rose after release of January sales data.
Dayton Hudson, whose same-store sales -- those from locations open at least a year -- rose 2.9%, adding 7/8 to 67 3/8; Gap, whose same-store sales climbed 3%, advanced 3/8 to 41 5/8, and Sears Roebuck, whose same-store sales jumped 7.9%, was up 1/4 to 51 1/2.
Woolworth, however, skidded 1 1/4 to 24 5/8.
The New York retailer estimated that its fourth-quarter earnings were "well below" their year-earlier level.
Its domestic same-store sales rose 2.2% in January.
Total sales for the month were off 7.2%.
Chiron (Nasdaq) declined 2 1/2 to 90 1/4 after Goldman Sachs & Co. analyst Maykin Ho downgraded the stock to "moderate outperformer" from "recommended for purchase."
She cited the stock's recent strength -- it set a 52-week high Monday -- as well as concerns about reimbursement for the Emeryville, Calif., company's recently approved multiple sclerosis drug.
Intel fell 2 to 63 1/4.
The Santa Clara, Calif., microchip company agreed to dismiss some patent infringement claims against Cyrix to partially settle litigation between the companies.
Shares of Cyrix, Richardson, Texas, jumped 3 1/8 to 26 1/8.
Both stocks trade on Nasdaq.
Telefonos de Mexico's American depositary receipts jumped 1 1/4 to 75 1/4 on speculation that the Mexican telephone company will report strong fourth-quarter results.
U.S. Surgical tumbled 2 7/8 to 28 1/4, in part on a cable-television report that said market sources predict shares of the Norwalk, Conn., medical-products company will sag to the low 20s.
Lancaster Colony (Nasdaq) dropped 2 to 43 1/4 even though the Columbus, Ohio, automotive and food-products manufacturer reported earnings of 68 cents a share for its fiscal second quarter ended Dec. 31, up from 53 cents a year earlier.
Flush with cash, institutional investors likely took advantage of the day's weakness to buy stocks late in the day, said David Shulman, a market strategist at Salomon Brothers.
Part of the concern about rising interest rates was linked to the scheduled release today of Labor Department data on January employment levels.
Some worry the Federal Reserve will move to tighten credit conditions if the report shows strength.
Economists expect an increase of about 186,000 in nonfarm payrolls.
Joseph McAlinden, chief investment officer at Dillon Read & Co., however, said a weaker-than-expected report on initial claims for unemployment insurance yesterday might mean today's data will come in at the low end of expectations.
