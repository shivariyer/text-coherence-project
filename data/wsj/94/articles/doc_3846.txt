A rebound in the bond market carried stocks mostly higher, even though the Dow Jones Industrial Average lost ground.
Although the industrial average posted its fourth straight decline, losing 6.76, or 0.18%, to 3832.02, broader indexes continued to take their cues from bonds.
The Treasury's 30-year bond gained ground for a second consecutive session after a dive on Thursday lifted its yield to the highest level since June.
The long bond was up 5/8 point, lowering its yield to 6.65%.
Helping to depress the industrial average was a sell-off in tobacco-related issues, including component issue Philip Morris, which tumbled 2 to 56.
Weakness in cyclical stocks had restrained the industrial average Friday.
The Nasdaq Stock Market, meanwhile, surged as investors barreled into communications-related stocks on news that MCI Communications plans to buy a stake in Nextel Communications as part of an alliance to offer wireless communications services.
Nextel climbed 5 5/8, or 14.6%, to 44 1/4.
The Nasdaq Composite Index advanced 8.72, or 1.11%, to 792.50.
Technology-related issues also were strong, including Microsoft, which rose 1 1/2 to 82 1/2, and 3Com, which added 2 5/8 to 60 5/8.
On the New York Stock Exchange, there were 1,457 issues advancing and 727 declining.
Volume slowed to 267,440,000 shares, from 273,220,000 shares Friday.
The Standard & Poor's 500 Stock Index increased 1.07, or 0.23%, to 467.14; the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 0.66, or 0.26%, to 259.23, and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index climbed 0.99, or 0.22%, to 442.74.
The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index jumped 3.89, or 0.83%, to 471.34.
Tobacco stocks were hurt by concerns about the potential for increased regulation in the cigarette business.
The Food and Drug Administration said there is growing evidence that tobacco companies deliberately maintain an addictive level of nicotine in cigarettes.
RJR Nabisco was the most active New York Stock Exchange issue, sliding 3/8 to 6 7/8 on volume of 5.5 million shares.
American Brands fell 5/8 to 33 1/8.
MCI Communications will buy a 17% stake in Nextel Communications as its initial purchase under an alliance among it, Nextel and Comcast.
The three companies expect to offer wireless personal communications services using technology developed by Motorola.
MCI shares added 7/8 to 27 3/8 and Comcast Class A rose 1 1/4 to 20 1/4, both also on Nasdaq.
Motorola retraced its early gains to end with a loss of 1 1/8 to 102 1/8.
Viacom Class B jumped 1 3/8 to 28 3/8 on the Amex, while Blockbuster Entertainment added 1/4 to 26 1/2 on the Big Board.
Some holders of Blockbuster, based in Fort Lauderdale, Fla., asked a Delaware judge to halt that company's planned merger with Viacom.
Many Blockbuster holders have complained they won't be paid enough for their shares.
Columbia/HCA rallied 2 1/8 to 43 after the Louisville, Ky., health-care provider reported fourth-quarter earnings of 52 cents a share, compared with earnings from continuing operations of 44 cents a share a year earlier.
Analysts surveyed by Institutional Brokers Estimate System had predicted a profit of 49 cents a share.
Coca-Cola Femsa added 1 5/8 to 32.
The Mexican soft drink company, a joint venture of Coca-Cola and Mexico's Femsa, reported that net income doubled in the fourth quarter.
Shares of Coca-Cola added 5/8 to 42 5/8.
First Chicago slipped 1/2 to 48 7/8, but its so-called DECS issue leapt 4 1/2 to 42 1/4.
The value of the DECS, or debt exchangeable for common stock, is tied to the big Chicago banking company's stake in Nextel.
The DECS were issued last month at 36 1/4.
Chiquita Brands rose 1 5/8 to 17 5/8 after the Cincinnati fresh fruit and vegetable concern reported a fourth-quarter loss of $1.17 a share.
That was narrower than the $2.97-a-share deficit it had from continuing operations a year earlier.
The company recorded restructuring charges last year.
Sofamor/Danek skidded 2 7/8 to 28.
An analyst at Smith Barney Shearson lowered an investment rating on shares of the Memphis, Tenn., maker of spinal implants to "neutral" from "outperform."
Marvel Entertainment climbed 1 7/8 to 25 3/8.
Merrill Lynch & Co. upgraded its intermediate-term rating on the New York comic book and trading card producer to "above average" from "neutral."
Coventry lost 1 5/8 to 50 3/8 on Nasdaq even though the Nashville, Tenn., company reported fourth-quarter earnings of 42 cents a share, after taking a four-cent-a-share charge tied to a lawsuit.
A year earlier, it had earnings of 33 cents a share.
"The market got moderately oversold last week and now it is trying to stabilize," said Philip Roth, a technical analyst at Dean Witter Reynolds.
"It looks to me like we are regrouping for another rally attempt," he said.
The industrial average lost 1.25% last week.
But analysts said the bond market will continue to set the course for stocks.
A test for both markets will come today when the Commerce Department revises its estimate of fourth-quarter gross domestic product.
Economists are expecting a 6.7% rate.
A surprisingly strong number likely would hurt stocks and bonds.
James Solloway, director of research at Argus Research, though, said he expects bond yields to drift lower this month.
That, combined with generally strong economic conditions and mild inflation, should help lift stocks, he said.
